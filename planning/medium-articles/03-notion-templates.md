# 10 Free Notion Templates That Actually Help You Get Things Done

*No fluff, no aesthetic-over-function nonsense — just templates that solve real productivity problems*

---

I've been using Notion daily for over two years. In that time, I've tried hundreds of templates — free ones, paid ones, viral ones with 50,000 downloads.

Most of them are beautiful and useless.

They look great in screenshots but fall apart in practice. Too many databases. Too many relations. Too many views that nobody ever uses. You spend more time maintaining the system than actually doing the work.

So I built my own. **10 templates focused on one thing: getting stuff done.** Each one solves a specific problem I actually had. No aesthetic filler. No 47-property databases. Just functional tools that take less than 5 minutes to set up.

Here they are.

---

## 1. Weekly Planner — The "One Page" System

**Problem it solves:** You have too many task management systems and none of them stick.

This template is a single page. That's it. Seven columns (Monday through Sunday), each with a simple to-do list. At the top: three "Big Rocks" for the week — the non-negotiable priorities.

**Why it works:** There's nothing to configure, no databases to maintain, and no views to toggle. You open it Monday morning, type your tasks, and check them off as the week progresses. Friday afternoon, you duplicate the page for next week and start fresh.

**Best for:** People who've tried (and abandoned) Todoist, Asana, TickTick, and every other task app.

---

## 2. Project Tracker — Kanban Without the Chaos

**Problem it solves:** You're juggling multiple projects and losing track of what's in progress.

A clean Kanban board with four columns: **Backlog → In Progress → Review → Done.** Each card has just three properties: priority (High/Medium/Low), due date, and a notes field.

**Why it works:** Most project trackers add dependencies, sub-tasks, sprint planning, and burn-down charts. Unless you're managing a 20-person engineering team, you don't need any of that. This template respects the fact that **you're probably a team of one** (or a very small team), and you just need to see what's where at a glance.

**Best for:** Freelancers, solo founders, and side hustlers managing 3-10 projects simultaneously.

---

## 3. Habit Tracker — The Streak Builder

**Problem it solves:** You want to build habits but can't stick with habit-tracking apps.

A monthly grid where each row is a habit and each column is a day. Check the box when you complete the habit. The template auto-calculates your **completion rate** and **current streak** for each habit.

**Why it works:** It's visual. Seeing a row of checkboxes fills you with a primal desire to not break the chain. Jerry Seinfeld's "Don't Break the Chain" method, built into Notion.

I track five habits: exercise, reading, language study, journaling, and one business task. Keeping it to five or fewer is key — **track too many habits and you'll track none.**

**Best for:** Anyone building new routines, especially remote workers who lack external structure.

---

## 4. Content Calendar — For Creators Who Actually Ship

**Problem it solves:** You have 50 content ideas but never publish consistently.

A calendar-view database with columns for: **Title, Platform, Status (Idea → Drafting → Scheduled → Published), Publish Date,** and **Notes.** That's it.

**Why it works:** The magic is in the **Status property.** Ideas are cheap. The bottleneck is always moving from "Idea" to "Published." This template makes that pipeline visible. When you see 30 ideas stuck in "Idea" status and nothing in "Drafting," you know exactly what needs to change.

I use this to manage content across three blogs, Medium, and social media. One database, one view, everything in one place.

**Best for:** Bloggers, YouTubers, newsletter writers, and anyone who creates content on a schedule.

---

## 5. Finance Dashboard — Know Your Numbers

**Problem it solves:** You don't know where your money goes each month.

A monthly expense tracker with categories (Housing, Food, Transport, Entertainment, Subscriptions, etc.) and a simple **income vs. expenses summary** at the top. Each month is a new database entry with a rollup showing your savings rate.

**Why it works:** It's not a replacement for Mint or YNAB. It's a **monthly check-in tool.** Spend 10 minutes at the end of each month entering your totals by category. Over time, you'll see trends — and trends drive better decisions.

After tracking for six months, I discovered I was spending $200/month on subscriptions I barely used. That's $2,400/year recovered in 10 minutes of work.

**Best for:** Anyone who wants financial awareness without the commitment of daily expense tracking.

## 6. Meeting Notes — Never Lose an Action Item Again

**Problem it solves:** You take meeting notes but never follow up on action items.

Each meeting note has four sections: **Attendees, Discussion Points, Decisions Made,** and **Action Items** (with owner and due date). Action items are a linked database, so they also appear in your Project Tracker.

**Why it works:** The linked database is the secret. Action items from meetings automatically flow into your task management system. No more "I think we discussed that but I can't find the note."

**Best for:** Anyone who has more than 3 meetings per week (so, basically everyone).

---

## 7. Reading List — Books That Don't Collect Dust

**Problem it solves:** You buy books faster than you read them and forget key takeaways.

A database with three views: **To Read** (sorted by priority), **Currently Reading** (max 2 at a time — this is enforced by guilt, not code), and **Completed** (with a notes field for key takeaways).

**Why it works:** The "Currently Reading" cap of 2 is crucial. **Reading 5 books simultaneously means finishing none of them.** And the takeaways field forces you to actually reflect on what you read instead of just consuming and forgetting.

**Best for:** Knowledge workers, lifelong learners, and anyone with an overflowing "To Read" shelf.

---

## 8. Goal Setter — Quarterly OKRs for Normal People

**Problem it solves:** You set ambitious goals on January 1st and forget them by February.

A quarterly goal-setting template using a simplified OKR (Objectives and Key Results) framework. Each quarter has 2-3 objectives, each with 2-3 measurable key results. A progress bar shows completion percentage.

**Why it works:** Annual goals are too distant. Weekly goals are too granular. **Quarterly goals hit the sweet spot** — long enough to achieve something meaningful, short enough to maintain urgency. The progress bar provides dopamine. The quarterly reset provides fresh starts.

**Best for:** Entrepreneurs, freelancers, and anyone who wants structured goal-setting without corporate overhead.

---

## 9. SOP Library — Document It Once, Do It Right Forever

**Problem it solves:** You do the same tasks repeatedly but slightly differently each time.

A searchable database of Standard Operating Procedures. Each SOP has: **Title, Category, Last Updated, and a step-by-step checklist.** I have SOPs for things like "Publish a blog post," "Set up a new API on RapidAPI," and "Monthly financial review."

**Why it works:** SOPs eliminate decision fatigue and ensure quality consistency. When you document a process, you can also delegate it — to a virtual assistant, a contractor, or even an automation tool. **If it's not documented, it's not delegatable.**

I currently have 25+ SOPs covering everything from content creation to server maintenance. Each one saves me 10-30 minutes of "how did I do this last time?" fumbling.

**Best for:** Solopreneurs, small team leads, and anyone who values systems over willpower.

---

## 10. Daily Journal — 5 Minutes to Clarity

**Problem it solves:** Your thoughts are a tangled mess and you never reflect on your days.

A daily entry template with three prompts:
- **What's my #1 priority today?** (forces focus)
- **What went well yesterday?** (builds gratitude)
- **What's one thing I'll do differently?** (drives improvement)

That's the whole template. Three questions. Takes 5 minutes.

**Why it works:** Complex journaling systems (morning pages, bullet journaling, gratitude lists with 10 items) have high dropout rates. Three focused questions are sustainable. I've maintained a daily streak for 8 months using this template, after failing at journaling for years.

**Best for:** Everyone. Seriously. If you do nothing else from this list, try the journal.

## A Few Tips for Using Notion Templates Effectively

### 1. Start With One Template
Don't download all 10 and set them up in one afternoon. Pick the one that solves your most painful problem right now. Use it for two weeks. Then add another.

### 2. Customize Ruthlessly
My templates are starting points. If a property doesn't serve you, delete it. If you need one I didn't include, add it. **The best system is one you'll actually use**, not one that looks good in a screenshot.

### 3. Review Weekly
Every Sunday, spend 15 minutes reviewing your Notion workspace. Archive completed projects, update your weekly planner, check your habit streaks. This weekly ritual is what keeps the system alive.

### 4. Keep It Simple
The moment your Notion setup feels like work, you've overcomplicated it. If you can't explain your system in 30 seconds, simplify it. Productivity tools should save time, not consume it.
