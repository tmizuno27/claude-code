# I Moved to Paraguay and My Cost of Living Dropped 70%

*Why South America's most overlooked country might be the best-kept secret for digital nomads and remote workers*

---

Two years ago, I was living in Southeast Asia — Malaysia, to be specific. Before that, Australia. Life was comfortable, but I kept running the numbers in my head. Even in "cheap" countries, costs were creeping up. Rent, international school fees for my two kids, groceries, health insurance — it all added up fast.

Then a friend mentioned Paraguay.

I laughed. Honestly, I couldn't even point to it on a map with confidence. But the more I researched, the more it made sense. **Today, I live in Asuncion, Paraguay's capital, and my total monthly expenses are roughly 70% lower than they were in Melbourne.**

Here's the full breakdown.

---

## Why Paraguay? (And Why Nobody Talks About It)

Paraguay is sandwiched between Argentina, Brazil, and Bolivia. It has a population of about 7 million, and most people outside South America know absolutely nothing about it.

**That's exactly why it's interesting.**

Unlike Portugal, Thailand, or Mexico — countries overrun by digital nomads and expats driving up prices — Paraguay is still genuinely affordable. There's no "expat premium." You pay what locals pay.

Here are the factors that caught my attention:

- **No natural disasters** — No earthquakes, hurricanes, tsunamis, or volcanic activity
- **Territorial tax system** — You're only taxed on income earned within Paraguay
- **Low cost of living** — Dramatically lower than most of the developed world
- **Warm climate year-round** — Subtropical, averaging 25-35°C
- **No hay fever** — Zero cedar pollen (this was a bigger deal for me than you'd think)
- **Easy residency** — Permanent residency is obtainable within months

## The Numbers: A Real Monthly Budget in Asuncion

Let me break down my actual monthly expenses for a family of three (myself and two school-age children). All figures in USD:

### Housing: $350/month
I rent a comfortable 3-bedroom house in a safe residential neighborhood. It has a yard, a garage, and is close to shops and schools. In Melbourne, a comparable house would be $2,500+/month. In Kuala Lumpur, around $800-1,000.

### International School: $300/month (per child)
My two kids attend a bilingual international school with English and Spanish instruction. Total: $600/month. The equivalent in Melbourne would be $2,000-3,000/month per child. In KL, about $800-1,200 per child.

### Groceries & Food: $250/month
Paraguay is a major agricultural country. **Beef here is world-class and incredibly cheap** — about $3-4/kg for quality cuts. Fresh fruits, vegetables, and dairy are all locally produced and affordable. Eating out at a local restaurant costs $3-5 per person for a full meal.

### Utilities: $80/month
Electricity, water, gas, and internet combined. Internet is fiber-optic and reliable (50+ Mbps). Electricity is cheap because Paraguay generates almost all its power from hydroelectric dams — specifically Itaipu, one of the largest in the world.

### Transportation: $100/month
Gas is affordable, and I own a used car (purchased for $5,000). No need for expensive public transit passes or ride-sharing. Parking is free almost everywhere.

### Health: $150/month
Private health insurance for the family. Doctor visits are $15-25 without insurance. Quality is good — many doctors trained in Brazil or Argentina.

### Miscellaneous: $150/month
Phone plans, entertainment, clothing, household items.

---

### Monthly Total: ~$1,680

Compare that to:
- **Melbourne, Australia**: ~$5,500-6,500/month (similar lifestyle)
- **Kuala Lumpur, Malaysia**: ~$2,800-3,500/month
- **Lisbon, Portugal**: ~$3,500-4,000/month
- **Mexico City, Mexico**: ~$2,500-3,000/month

**Paraguay wins by a landslide.** And this isn't a stripped-down, rice-and-beans budget. This is a comfortable life with international school, a private house, a car, and health insurance.

## The Tax Situation: Almost Too Good to Be True

Paraguay uses a **territorial tax system.** This means:

- Income earned outside Paraguay? **Not taxed.**
- No capital gains tax on foreign investments
- Corporate tax: flat 10%
- Personal income tax: flat 10% (only on local income)
- VAT: 10%

For remote workers, freelancers, and digital entrepreneurs earning from international clients, this is enormous. **Your foreign-sourced income is legally tax-free in Paraguay.**

I'm not a tax advisor, and you should absolutely consult one for your specific situation. But the territorial system is a legitimate, well-established framework — not a loophole.

## Getting Residency: Easier Than You'd Expect

Paraguay offers one of the most straightforward permanent residency processes in the world:

1. **Deposit ~$5,000** in a Paraguayan bank account
2. **Submit documents** — birth certificate, police clearance, health certificate (all apostilled)
3. **Wait 2-4 months** for processing
4. **Receive permanent residency** — no renewal required

That's it. No investment visa with a $500,000 minimum. No points system. No sponsorship needed. You deposit a modest amount, submit your paperwork, and you're a permanent resident.

After 3 years of residency, you can apply for citizenship (and Paraguay allows dual citizenship).

## The Honest Downsides

I'm not going to pretend Paraguay is paradise. Here's what you need to know:

### Language Barrier
Spanish is essential for daily life. Most people do not speak English. **If you don't speak Spanish, your first few months will be challenging.** I use AI translation tools daily, and I'm slowly improving, but it's a real hurdle.

### Infrastructure
Roads outside Asuncion can be rough. Bureaucracy is slow. Things that take 5 minutes online in Japan or Australia take a personal visit and 2 hours here. Patience is non-negotiable.

### Limited Entertainment
There are no world-class museums, concert halls, or Michelin-star restaurants. If you need constant urban stimulation, Asuncion will bore you. But if you're content with nature, barbecues with friends, and a slower pace of life — it's wonderful.

### Summer Heat
December through February (summer in the Southern Hemisphere) is brutal. 40°C+ with high humidity. Air conditioning is not a luxury — it's survival equipment.

### Distance from Everything
Paraguay is far from, well, everywhere. Flights to North America, Europe, or Asia are long and expensive. If you need to travel internationally often, factor in flight costs and 20+ hour travel times.

## A Day in My Life in Asuncion

**7:00 AM** — Wake up, make coffee. The morning is cool and quiet. Birds are loud.

**7:30 AM** — Drive the kids to school (10 minutes, no traffic).

**8:00 AM - 12:00 PM** — Work from my home office. Fiber internet, no interruptions. I run several online businesses — affiliate websites, API products, and digital template sales.

**12:00 PM** — Lunch. Usually something simple at home, or a $4 almuerzo ejecutivo (business lunch) at a nearby restaurant — soup, main course, drink, and dessert included.

**1:00 PM - 5:00 PM** — More work, or errands. The supermarket is 5 minutes away. Everything is close.

**5:00 PM** — Pick up the kids. They're becoming fluent in Spanish, which amazes me daily.

**6:00 PM** — Asado (barbecue) in the backyard, or a simple home-cooked dinner.

**8:00 PM** — Kids to bed. I read, plan the next day, or work on side projects.

It's not glamorous. But it's peaceful, affordable, and gives me something I never had in expensive cities: **financial breathing room.**

## Who Should Consider Paraguay?

Paraguay is ideal if you:

- Work remotely and earn in USD/EUR/GBP
- Want to dramatically reduce expenses without sacrificing comfort
- Have kids and want affordable international schooling
- Don't need a bustling nightlife or expat social scene
- Are comfortable learning Spanish (or willing to start)
- Value financial freedom over Instagram-worthy locations

It's **not** ideal if you:

- Need to be close to a major international hub
- Can't tolerate heat and humidity
- Require English-speaking environments
- Want a large, established expat community

Moving to Paraguay was the single best financial decision I've made. **Dropping my cost of living by 70% gave me the runway to build businesses without the pressure of a high burn rate.**

If you're a remote worker doing the mental math on cost of living, put Paraguay on your list. It deserves to be there.
